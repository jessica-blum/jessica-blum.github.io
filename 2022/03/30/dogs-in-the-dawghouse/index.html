Dogs in the Dawghouse

2022-03-30

Gonzaga left the tournament with tails stuch between their hind legs.

If you couldn’t already tell, Gonzaga was not the champion of the NCAA March Madness Tournament this year. If they were, this would be more of a joyful piece in which you could take what I did and maybe recreate it for yourself.

However, I would advise that only bits and pieces of work may be worth taking up in future work. I did end up in the top 85th percentile with my bracket, the best out of the eight brackets I produced through ESPN for this year’s slate of games.

In all honesty, I watch basketball, I love reporting and talking about the numbers, but I have never cared for basketball in a way that I have thought about what makes a team a team, what statistics are going to tell the story of two teams coming into a game in the tournament and will tell me that this team will win. Good basketball teams to me are creating turnovers AND are getting points off of those turnovers. Good basketball teams are out-rebounding the other team, specifically on offense if they can. Good basketball teams are making about a third of their three-point attempts, if not more.

To me, I find myself wanting to chase after a really decent accuracy. My goal is to reach somewhere in the 80 percent range. If my accuracy is that high, I am more likely to get more than 3 out of 4 games right. Who doesn’t want a successful bracket? I will live and die by a model that could get me 80 percent and not be overfit/too correlated. So, I set out to try various combinations of statistics to find the best predictors from what I can work with.

At first, I was trying three different machine learning tools, seeing where one may beat out the others. What I came to find after about four to five runs of different predictors was that logistic regression always, for me, out-performed support vector machines and random forest with accuracy, and it took less time to run and get results. That was a win-win situation in my eyes, so I could focus on the combinations, such as with the predictors and rolling windows, in order to get more predictive power.

I also took more time to experiment with rolling vs. cumulative statistics which did not vary much from each other from what we did in class, but what if some predictors were cumulative, and some were rolling? What if the size of the window changes for each predictor? I set out wanting to answer these questions, but it could be a tall task. What I found was there were only slightly better results with a rolling vs. cumulative statistic oF, for example, offensive efficiency. In the end, I made most of my variables rolling and chose different window sizes that I thought would help tell the story. If a metric would see little variation game to game, like free throw factor, I kept the rolling window to 10, but if I saw more variation game to game, I wanted to keep the window smaller. In that case, I chose a smaller rolling window of about 3 games with metrics like rolling steals or blocks or true shooting percentage.

My thought process in thinking about just straight up adding like three-point shots made is that that one number from a game is not very indicative of what the team may do in the next game. Hence, that’s why rolling metrics are supposed to be predictive because they average the past few games together to try to be more helpful in predicting the next game. So, in thinking that, I removed all the stats from above that were not rolling and that were not KenPom data (I can’t roll KenPom data) and made them into rolling metrics which gave me a more modest model.

Alabama was the one major disappointment from this round of games. The two games that ended up being incorrect though were ones that my model was the most unsure about. Alabama had just a 62 percent chance of winning. In terms of one problem Alabama faced in that game, their offensive efficiency was poor at 88.9 compared to their season cumulative of approximately 108.7. That stat represents how a team produces out of 100 possessions, and Notre Dame was outscoring especially in the second half. Another reason was that Alabama was shooting 33 percent from beyond the arc, connecting on 8 of 24 shots. Notre Dame shot 62 percent in terms of 3-pointers, making 10 of 16.

Other than those two missed predictions for the round of 64, the round of 32 went swimmingly. My model predicted no upsets, which was dangerous, but it turned out.

The Sweet 16 is where success eluded me. Within this region, my championship team (Gonzaga) lost by six points to Arkansas, and Texas Tech failed to boot Coach K from the tournament.

Past this round, my model only predicted three of the eight teams to make it to the Elite 8. None of those teams were in the West Region.

As my model also concurs, the Big Ten was supposed to crash and burn. Early. However, a notable loss for me in the round of 32 in this region was when Tennessee fell to Michigan, 76-68. Tennessee was an Elite 8 team in my model’s mind, but that was not what verified on the floor. Some of those exposed numbers from that game included a horrendous show of 3-point shooting. The Wolverines were 6 of 16 to make a 37.5 percent shooting statistic from long range. That was the better stat line. Tennesee actually made 2 of 18 attempts. Michigan turned the ball over 14 times compared to Tennessee’s just 6 turnovers. In one of my predictors, offensive efficiency, Michigan outperformed with a rating of 108.6 to the Volunteers’ 97.1.

Three of the four teams of this region I did predict to make it to the Sweet 16, but I did not predict Arizona to fall in that round. Arizona was another team that did not show up against Houston, losing by 12 points. Arizona was my team to go to the Final Four from this part of the bracket.

The closest misses were games that teetered on the edge of any sort of confidence. LSU only had a 53 percent chance of winning their game against the Cyclones, and USC only had a 55 percent chance of winning against Miami. I wish I had time to go back through and make a bracket for fun just choosing all the OPPOSITE teams when my model was on the verge of picking the opponent. I think I would have been surprised by the results.

The Miami game turned out to be close, too. They only won by two points, but their turnover percentage was 4 percent compared to USC’s 24 percent, an estimate of turnovers per every 100 plays. The game was sloppy; Miami made 1 of 14 attempts for three, and both teams were awful from a free throw percentage viewpoint, 76 percent and 60 percent by USC. Miami turned out to shake off the early jitters in this game, looking strong the rest of the tournament.

The final four teams this year were Duke, North Carolina, Villanova and Kansas. Two second seeded teams, an eight seed, and a top seed. Since my model wasn’t one for upsets, I never liked that my model predicted all of the top seeds to go to the Final Four. In the end, Kansas was the only one to hold up for me so Rock Chalk and all of that. The Jayhawks single-handedly managed to pull me out of about sixth place back into the fight.

Final Four, One for Four

Once all the potential points were added up and no more were left to be claimed, my final position was… second.

If I were to have continued my work and had more time, I would have tried to incorporate more outside factors than the stats recorded between buzzers. The few things I can think of that would have mattered going into the game include previous tournament experience among players on a team, injuries and a bench factor that looked into who a team may have had coming off of the bench to contribute.

All is well that ends well, and I will take a second-place finish and Coach K not seeing the light of day in the championship game.
