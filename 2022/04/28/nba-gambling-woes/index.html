There comes a time in one’s life that the lure of betting tempts the brain and gives one the feeling of accomplishment or entertainment. For some, that comes with turning of age and seeing others do it. For some, it can be the location of a vacation such as Las Vegas.

For me, the lure never came. For me, I HAD to start betting because of a class in college.

Sure, my spring break led me right into the jaws of Downtown Las Vegas, but at that time, I got satisfaction from watching everyone else blow their money as I strolled by without betting a penny. This semester, I have learned and thought about betting more than any other period in my life.

My class in college was an advanced sports data class. The first half was spent building a model to predict the NCAA March Madness Tournament. Naturally, the second half’s purpose was to build a model to pull in NBA data and tie in moneyline odds.

I worked with two other people, Brett and Collin, to build a model within a programming language called R. We used a sportsdataverse website and their hoopR package to manipulate up-to-date statistics on NBA teams and predict the outcome. Our professor set the starting wages at $1500. It was up to each group to try to make money long enough to stay afloat during the betting period. Each day had a minimum bet amount based on how many games were being played.

The Man (and Woman)

What was your human intervention approach? How did you collectively arrive at your decisions?

There was a human element to our approach when placing daily bets. Using a group message, we ran our model each day to see updated moneylines for the games. Based on those numbers, we then looked more into the makeup of each team manually. Various teams were facing injuries, sicknesses, had poor performances the night before that may motivate them the next day, etc. There are a lot of outside factors that can influence a game that NBA statistics will not account for when making a prediction.
